Learning Outcomes

  • Use the sociological imagination to connect personal experience with public issues.
  • Explain how culture and socialisation shape values and behaviour.
  • Describe core institutions (family, education, economy, state, religion) and their roles.
  • Compare basic methods (qualitative/quantitative) and evaluate evidence quality.
  • Discuss inequality across class, gender, race/ethnicity, and how it persists or changes.

Indicative Topics

Sociology’s Questions

What is social order? How do norms emerge? Why do societies change? Micro vs macro viewpoints.

Culture & Socialisation

Symbols, values, norms; primary/secondary socialisation; roles, identity, deviance & stigma.

Theory (Foundations)

Durkheim (solidarity), Marx (class & conflict), Weber (rationalisation), and later perspectives.

Methods & Ethics

Surveys/experiments, interviews/ethnography, sampling, bias, reliability/validity, research ethics.

Institutions & Inequality

Family, education, economy, state, religion; stratification and mobility; social policy basics.

Quick Reference

Four lenses (at a glance)

Functionalism     → stability & roles of institutions
Conflict theory   → power, class, inequality, change
Symbolic interaction → meaning-making in micro-interactions
Rational choice   → incentives & constraints on actors

Real explanations often combine multiple lenses.

Evaluating evidence

Internal validity: did X cause Y?
External validity: does it generalise?
Measurement: is it reliable & valid?
Bias: selection, response, observer effects

Correlation ≠ causation; look for counterfactuals or controls.

Key terms

  • Norms — expected behaviours; sanctions enforce them.
  • Role — behaviour expected of a status (e.g., student, parent).
  • Social stratification — structured inequality in resources/prestige.

Mini-application

Question: Why do some policies face resistance?
Lens: norms + interests + narratives
Approach: map actors, incentives, and cultural frames;
look for unintended consequences.

Key Takeaways

  • Personal troubles are often public issues rooted in structures.
  • Evidence quality matters: methods, measures, and ethics shape conclusions.
  • Institutions coordinate life but can also reproduce inequalities.
